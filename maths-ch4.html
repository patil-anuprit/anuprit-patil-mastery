1. Introduction

A quadratic equation is an equation of degree 2. The highest power of the variable is always 2. Quadratic equations appear in mathematics, engineering, physics, architecture, computer graphics, economics and many real-life situations.

ax² + bx + c = 0

where a ≠ 0 b and c are real numbers x is the variable

Examples

  • x² + 5x + 6 = 0
  • 2x² − 3x + 1 = 0
  • 5x² − 20 = 0
  • 7x² + x − 9 = 0

Non Examples

  • x + 5 = 0
  • 3x³ + 4x + 1 = 0
  • √x + 2 = 0

2. Standard Form of a Quadratic Equation

Every quadratic equation can be written in the standard form:

a x² + b x + c = 0

where:

  • a = coefficient of x²
  • b = coefficient of x
  • c = constant term
  • a ≠ 0

Examples

  • x² + 5x + 6 = 0
  • 2x² − 3x + 1 = 0
  • 4x² − 25 = 0
  • 7x² + x − 8 = 0

Convert to Standard Form

Example 1:

x² + 5x = 6

x² + 5x − 6 = 0

Example 2:

(x + 2)(x + 3) = 0

x² + 5x + 6 = 0

3. Discriminant and Nature of Roots

One of the most important concepts in quadratic equations is the Discriminant.

D = b² − 4ac

The discriminant helps us predict the nature of roots without solving the equation.

Case 1 : D > 0

The equation has two distinct real roots.

Example: x² − 5x + 6 = 0 D = 25 − 24 = 1 Since D > 0, there are two different real roots.

Case 2 : D = 0

The equation has equal roots.

Example: x² − 6x + 9 = 0 D = 36 − 36 = 0 Hence both roots are equal.

Case 3 : D < 0

The equation has no real roots.

Example: x² + x + 1 = 0 D = 1 − 4 = -3 Since D is negative, no real roots exist.

Quick Summary

  • D > 0 → Two distinct real roots
  • D = 0 → Equal roots
  • D < 0 → No real roots

4. Methods of Solving Quadratic Equations

A quadratic equation can be solved by different methods. The method used depends on the form of the equation and the convenience of calculation.

Method 1 : Factorisation Method

In this method, the quadratic equation is expressed as a product of two linear factors.

x² + 5x + 6 = 0

x² + 2x + 3x + 6 = 0 x(x + 2) + 3(x + 2) = 0 (x + 2)(x + 3) = 0

Therefore, x = -2 or x = -3

Method 2 : Completing the Square

In this method, the quadratic equation is converted into a perfect square.

x² + 6x + 5 = 0

x² + 6x = -5 x² + 6x + 9 = 4 (x + 3)² = 4 x + 3 = ±2 x = -1 or x = -5

Method 3 : Quadratic Formula

This method can solve every quadratic equation.

x = (-b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a

Substitute the values of a, b and c into the formula to obtain the roots.

Comparison of Methods

  • Factorisation → Fast when factors are easy to find.
  • Completing the Square → Useful for understanding the structure of equations.
  • Quadratic Formula → Works for every quadratic equation.

5. Formation of Quadratic Equations from Given Roots

Sometimes the roots of a quadratic equation are given and we need to form the quadratic equation. This concept is very important because it connects the roots of an equation with its coefficients. Many CBSE Board questions are based on this idea.

Basic Formula

If α and β are the roots of a quadratic equation, then:

x² − (α + β)x + αβ = 0

This formula is obtained using the relationship between roots and coefficients.

Example 1

Form the quadratic equation whose roots are 2 and 5.

α = 2 β = 5

Sum of roots = 2 + 5 = 7

Product of roots = 2 × 5 = 10

Substituting into the formula:

x² − 7x + 10 = 0

Therefore, the required quadratic equation is:

x² − 7x + 10 = 0

Example 2

Form the quadratic equation whose roots are 3 and −4.

Sum of roots = 3 + (−4) = −1

Product of roots = 3 × (−4) = −12

Substituting into the formula:

x² + x − 12 = 0

Example 3

Form the quadratic equation whose roots are 1/2 and 3.

Sum of roots = 1/2 + 3 = 7/2

Product of roots = 3/2

Equation:

x² − (7/2)x + (3/2) = 0

Multiplying throughout by 2:

2x² − 7x + 3 = 0

Important Exam Tips

  • Always calculate the sum of roots first.
  • Then calculate the product of roots.
  • Substitute carefully into the formula.
  • Watch the signs when negative roots are involved.
  • If fractions appear, multiply the equation by the denominator to remove fractions.

Common Mistakes Students Make

  • Using the wrong sign before the sum of roots.
  • Forgetting that αβ means multiplication.
  • Making sign errors with negative roots.
  • Leaving fractional coefficients when simplification is expected.

Quadratic Equation:

6. Relationship Between Zeroes and Coefficients

This is one of the most important concepts in Quadratic Equations. It establishes a direct relationship between the roots (zeroes) of a quadratic equation and its coefficients. This topic is frequently tested in CBSE Board Exams, Sample Papers and Competency-Based Questions.

ax² + bx + c = 0

If α and β are the zeroes (roots) of the equation, then:

α + β = -b/a
αβ = c/a

These two formulas allow us to find the sum and product of roots without actually solving the equation.

Meaning of the Formula

  • α + β gives the sum of roots.
  • αβ gives the product of roots.
  • a, b and c are coefficients of the quadratic equation.
  • This relationship works for every quadratic equation.

Example 1

Find the sum and product of roots of:

x² - 7x + 12 = 0

Here, a = 1 b = -7 c = 12

Sum of roots:

α + β = -(-7)/1 = 7

Product of roots:

αβ = 12/1 = 12

Example 2

2x² + 5x - 3 = 0

Here, a = 2 b = 5 c = -3

Sum of roots:

α + β = -5/2

Product of roots:

αβ = -3/2

Example 3

3x² - x - 4 = 0

Here, a = 3 b = -1 c = -4

Sum of roots:

α + β = 1/3

Product of roots:

αβ = -4/3

Board Exam Type Question

The sum of zeroes of a quadratic polynomial is 6 and the product of zeroes is 8. Find the quadratic polynomial.

Using:

x² - (Sum)x + Product = 0

Substituting:

x² - 6x + 8 = 0

Common Mistakes

  • Forgetting the negative sign in -b/a.
  • Using b/a instead of -b/a.
  • Confusing coefficients with roots.
  • Incorrect substitution of signs.
  • Mixing up sum and product formulas.

Quick Revision Box

For ax² + bx + c = 0

Sum of Roots = α + β = -b/a

Product of Roots = αβ = c/a

Memory Trick

Remember: "SUM takes the SIGN change." "PRODUCT keeps the CONSTANT." This helps recall: α + β = -b/a αβ = c/a

7. Graph of a Quadratic Equation (Parabola)

The graph of every quadratic equation is called a Parabola. A parabola is a smooth U-shaped or inverted U-shaped curve. The shape depends upon the coefficient of x².

y = ax² + bx + c

Case 1 : a > 0

When the coefficient of x² is positive, the parabola opens upward. Example:

y = x²

The graph looks like a U-shape. The vertex represents the minimum point.

Case 2 : a < 0

When the coefficient of x² is negative, the parabola opens downward. Example:

y = -x²

The graph looks like an inverted U. The vertex represents the maximum point.

Important Parts of a Parabola

  • Vertex → Highest or lowest point of the graph.
  • Axis of Symmetry → Vertical line passing through the vertex.
  • X-intercepts → Points where the graph cuts the x-axis.
  • Y-intercept → Point where the graph cuts the y-axis.

Connection Between Graph and Roots

The roots of a quadratic equation are the points where the parabola intersects the x-axis. Therefore the graph can tell us the nature of roots.

If D > 0

The parabola cuts the x-axis at two different points. Therefore there are two distinct real roots.

If D = 0

The parabola touches the x-axis at exactly one point. Therefore there are equal roots.

If D < 0

The parabola does not touch the x-axis. Therefore no real roots exist.

Example

y = x² - 5x + 6

Factorising:

(x - 2)(x - 3) = 0

Roots are: x = 2 and x = 3 Therefore the graph cuts the x-axis at x = 2 and x = 3.

Quick Revision Box

a > 0 → Parabola opens upward

a < 0 → Parabola opens downward

D > 0 → Two x-intercepts

D = 0 → One x-intercept

D < 0 → No x-intercept

8. Previous Year Questions (PYQs) + Important Board Questions

This section contains CBSE exam-focused questions based on Quadratic Equations. These are frequently asked in board exams and sample papers.

Question 1 (Basic Factorisation)

Solve: x² − 7x + 12 = 0

Solution:

x² − 7x + 12 = 0 (x − 3)(x − 4) = 0 x = 3 or x = 4

Question 2 (Discriminant Based)

Find nature of roots: x² + 4x + 5 = 0

Solution:

D = b² − 4ac = 16 − 20 = −4

Since D < 0, no real roots exist.

Question 3 (Quadratic Formula)

Solve: 2x² − 3x − 2 = 0

Solution:

x = (-b ± √(b² − 4ac)) / 2a

a = 2, b = −3, c = −2

x = (3 ± √9 + 16) / 4 x = (3 ± √25) / 4 x = (3 ± 5) / 4

x = 2 or x = −1/2

Question 4 (Formation of Equation)

Form quadratic equation with roots 1 and −6

Solution:

Sum = −5 Product = −6

x² + 5x − 6 = 0

Question 5 (Word Problem)

The product of two consecutive numbers is 56. Find the numbers.

Solution:

Let numbers be x and x+1 x(x+1) = 56 x² + x − 56 = 0 (x + 8)(x − 7) = 0

x = 7 or x = −8

Numbers = (7, 8)

9. HOTS + Tricky Exam Questions (Advanced Level)

This section contains Higher Order Thinking Skills (HOTS) questions based on quadratic equations. These questions test deep understanding and are commonly used in CBSE competency-based exams.

Question 1 (Concept + Trick)

If one root of the equation x² − 5x + k = 0 is 2, find k and the other root.

Solution:

Substitute x = 2:

4 − 10 + k = 0

k = 6

Now equation becomes:

x² − 5x + 6 = 0

Other root = 3

Question 2 (Sum-Product Trick)

If α and β are roots such that α + β = 7 and αβ = 10, form the equation.

Solution:

x² − 7x + 10 = 0

Question 3 (Condition Based)

Find the value of k if the equation x² + kx + 9 = 0 has equal roots.

Solution:

For equal roots:

D = 0
k² − 36 = 0

k = ±6

Question 4 (Hidden Factorisation)

Solve: x² − 2x − 15 = 0

Solution:

(x − 5)(x + 3) = 0

x = 5 or x = −3

Question 5 (Real Life Application)

The area of a rectangle is 48 m². Its length is 2 m more than its breadth. Find dimensions.

Solution:

Let breadth = x, length = x + 2

x(x + 2) = 48
x² + 2x − 48 = 0

x = 6 or x = −8 (reject negative)

Breadth = 6, Length = 8
